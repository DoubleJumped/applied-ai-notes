The Premise

The bridge
across the aisle.

Every company can now buy intelligence. The same frontier models are available to anyone willing to pay, and every enterprise is already reaching for the same stack. If the capability is universal, then the capability cannot be the advantage.

The edge moves elsewhere — into deployment. Into where, how, and why a particular business puts intelligence to work, and, just as often, where it refuses to. Someone has to stand at the seam between the messy reality of the work and the clean promise of the model, and decide what belongs on each side.

That person is the forward deployed engineer — the best combination of consultant and engineer, not the average of the two. Their job is to turn a genuine understanding of the business into working software that carries real responsibility, end to end.

What follows is that job as a working playbook: audit, build, evals, deploy — then watch, improve, and run the loop again.

A feature on the discipline of bringing artificial intelligence into a real business — and the person who owns the path from a messy workflow to a system the company will trust.

The Role

Consultant and engineer —
not the average of the two.

Business reality is how the work actually happens today — the tools, the people, the exceptions, the incentives, the quiet politics. FDE judgment is the ruling on where intelligence belongs and where it does not. Between them sits a deployed system: working software carrying real responsibility inside the company.

The business side is consulting muscle — workflows, cost, risk, adoption, and the art of extracting information people don't know they have. The technical side is engineering muscle — models, APIs, data, reliability, evals, guardrails, harnesses. The rare thing is not either half. It is one person fluent in both.

Judgment lives at the bridge. "This design gives eighty percent accuracy and isn't worth the risk; this other workflow has higher return, lower risk, and ships faster." That sentence is the whole job. FDEs are in demand precisely because they control how intelligence enters the business — the one decision no model can make for itself.

“If you can speak both art and science, you can do this job.

Most people start strong on one side and must deliberately build the other. The bridge is not a compromise — it is the best of each, kept intact.

Phase One — Discovery

The documented process is rarely the real one.

Ask someone for step one and you will hear, "an email arrives." Watch instead, and every simple step turns out to hide the real work. The trigger is forty-odd senders, no two formatted alike, half of them exceptions in disguise — "same as last time," "Sarah already signed off" — with the routing logic living entirely in one person's head.

Those unwritten rules for what counts are the specification. If you do not look, you build for a system that does not map to reality — which is how most AI pilots quietly fail.

The spreadsheet is a database, a workflow engine, and an audit log wearing a grid costume. Reverse-engineer all three before you replace any.

The Deployment Issue·July 2026Phase One — page 07
Phase One — Discovery

Sit with the work,
not the org chart.

“In an hour, people describe the job they think they have.

Beside them for eight, you see the undocumented exception actually happen. Discovery is also where trust is built — the relationship is infrastructure for everything that follows.

Shadow full days. A one-hour interview buys you the story people tell about their work. Eight hours at the desk buys you the work itself — especially the exception nobody wrote down.

Get system access. The ERP, the CRM, the four spreadsheets, the Slack channels where approvals actually live. Read the real artifacts, never the SOP.

Expect multiple owners. In any large organization several people run the "same" process differently. One source is a sample size of one — sit with more.

Chase the exceptions. There is one way for the work to go right and a thousand ways to go wrong. Capture the tribal knowledge before it walks out the door.

The Deployment Issue·July 2026Phase Two — page 09
Phase Two — The Audit

What to automate — and what to leave alone.

i.

Deterministic software

When the rules and inputs are predictable. Most of a good solution is this — don't spend a model on an if-statement.

ii.

An agent

When the objective is clear but the inputs, path, or actions vary. Often only three steps of ten actually need judgment.

iii.

A human in control

When the decision carries material ambiguity, accountability, or irreversible consequences.

The only three things a business measures

Revenue uplift

Reach customers faster, drive more value per customer, unlock throughput the old process capped.

Risk mitigation

Fewer errors, auditable decisions, escalation by rule — the failures that were silent now leave evidence.

Cost savings

Hours returned, rework eliminated, cost per run measured and driven relentlessly down.

“Every expected-value claim lands in one of these three — quantified, not vibes.

Chapter Two — The Build

Build for the unhappy paths.

There is one way a thing goes right and a thousand ways it goes wrong. An agent built for the happy path is worth nothing; the one that handles the exceptions is the one worth deploying.

The Deployment Issue·July 2026Phase Three — page 11
Phase Three — The Build

Add capability in this order.

01 — Agent loop

The engine

Prompt → model → response → next step, until done or a max-step limit hits.

02 — Tool use

Reach out

Start minimal — one API call, one search. The agent decides when to call them.

03 — Guardrails

Fence it in

Input validation, a max-step limit, output filtering. The safe operating envelope.

04 — Audit trail

Show your work

Log every prompt, tool call, result, error, timestamp. No visible traces, no trust.

05 — Structured output

A contract

A defined JSON schema, validated on every response. Retry or escalate on mismatch.

06 — State & recovery

Survive a kill

Checkpoint every few steps. Kill it deliberately — it must resume where it stopped.

07 — Failure handling

Name the modes

Missing data, dead APIs, timeouts, duplicates — enumerate each and define the behavior.

08 — Retries & backoff

Try again, wisely

Exponential on every external call: 1s · 2s · 4s · 8s, capped. Escalate when they run out.

“No visible traces, no trust.

Pick one model and one agent platform and get genuinely good at it. Use cheaper models for simple subtasks and cache to manage cost — but your value is judgment about the workflow, not the inference layer.

Phase Five — Deployment

A people problem wearing a software costume.

Never open with "first, migrate off your ERP." A company that spent years and millions moving onto a platform will tell you to get lost. Build on top of the current stack and connect it — that is the edge over every rip-and-replace vendor.

Test inside a sandbox in the company's own infrastructure, where you can run, inspect, and debug safely. Then increase autonomy gradually: shadow mode → approve every action → approve only the risky ones → autonomy where earned. Begin with the smallest useful action; grant authority only after reliability is proven.

Two political realities are worth engineering around. You are a risk to your sponsor — the status quo is safe, and bringing you in is not; if it fails, it is their name on it. So de-risk them, visibly. And your real job is to get that sponsor promoted: drive value in the three buckets, make it attributable, and at review time they can point to it. Face to face makes every hard conversation easier. The relationship is infrastructure.

01Integrate with what exists

Build on top of the current stack. That's the edge over rip-and-replace.

02Test in a controlled space

A sandbox inside their own infrastructure — run, inspect, debug safely.

03Earn autonomy gradually

Smallest useful action first. Authority follows proven reliability, never precedes it.

“When it breaks, it's your name on the line.
